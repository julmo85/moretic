Citizen-led project in progress

Architectural Autonomy Without Borders

A practical field guide to real-life accessibility in Fribourg: routes, accessible toilets, disabled parking spaces, cultural venues, obstacles, rest areas and useful information before going out.

Français English Deutsch
View planned pilot sheets View identified sources

1. General idea

The project “Architectural Autonomy Without Borders” aims to better document real-life accessibility in Fribourg.

It is primarily intended for people with disabilities or reduced mobility, but it may also be useful for older people, caregivers, companions, parents with strollers, temporarily injured people, or anyone who needs reliable information to move around with more confidence.

The goal is not to replace existing official information, but to complement it with a practical approach based on the real needs of the people concerned.

A truly accessible city is not only about having a ramp or an accessibility pictogram. It is also about being able to arrive, enter, move around, use accessible toilets, rest, ask for help, leave safely and anticipate obstacles.

2. Starting point

Some accessibility information already exists, but it is often scattered, incomplete or too general to help someone properly prepare an outing.

Before going out, a person may need to know:

  • how to get there;
  • where to park;
  • which bus stop to use;
  • whether there are slopes or cobblestones;
  • how to enter;
  • whether there is a lift;
  • where to find accessible toilets;
  • where to rest;
  • who to contact if needed;
  • whether there is a backup plan in case of an obstacle.

3. What the project adds

The project proposes to:

  • centralize useful information;
  • identify practical gaps;
  • document a few concrete places and routes;
  • distinguish between official sources, field observations and information that still needs to be checked;
  • suggest realistic improvements;
  • encourage contributions from people concerned;
  • support collaboration between associations, institutions, tourism actors and public authorities.

4. Planned pilot sheets

To start in a realistic and concrete way, the project could focus on 3 or 4 pilot sheets. These first sheets would demonstrate the usefulness of the approach without trying to document the whole city from the start.

Pilot sheet 1

Fribourg railway station → city centre

Show whether the route is actually usable, including slopes, cobblestones, difficult crossings, useful bus stops and rest areas.

Pilot sheet 2

Accessible public toilet

Check location, access, opening hours, possible Eurokey access, route to the toilet and real usefulness during an outing.

Pilot sheet 3

Disabled parking space near an important place

Document the parking space, distance to the destination, slopes, cobblestones, pavement access and possible alternatives.

Pilot sheet 4

Museum or frequently visited cultural venue

Compare published information with real-life needs: entrance, indoor circulation, lift, toilets, resting places, contact, route and useful photos.

5. Proposed format

The project could first take the form of a simple, accessible and evolving website.

The website could include a project presentation page, practical sheets for places, routes or services, downloadable accessible PDF sheets, a list of official resources, a citizen contribution form, a few documented pilot sheets and a simple map in a second phase.

In the medium term, a lightweight map could be considered, possibly connected to existing tools such as OpenStreetMap, Wheelmap, Ginto or other accessibility platforms.

6. Citizen contribution

The project could allow people concerned, relatives, caregivers, associations or users to submit practical information.

  • an obstacle;
  • an accessible place;
  • incorrect information;
  • missing information;
  • an accessible toilet;
  • a disabled parking space;
  • a difficult route;
  • a broken lift;
  • roadworks or temporary obstacles.

7. Sustainability and governance

For the project to remain useful over time, a simple form of governance will be needed.

  • independent and volunteer-based project at the beginning;
  • partnership with an association;
  • small citizen review committee;
  • occasional support from institutions;
  • funded pilot project;
  • collaboration with the City, Fribourg Tourism or cultural actors.

8. Accessibility of the guide itself

  • good contrast
  • readable text
  • simple navigation
  • mobile compatibility
  • keyboard navigation
  • alternative text for images
  • accessible PDF documents
  • clear language
  • fast loading

10. Measuring impact

  • number of sheets created;
  • number of places documented;
  • number of routes documented;
  • number of accessible toilets listed;
  • number of disabled parking spaces documented;
  • number of contributions received;
  • number of corrections made;
  • user feedback;
  • testimonials from people concerned;
  • collaborations obtained;
  • concrete improvements made by places or institutions.

11. Contact and collaboration

This project is independent, citizen-led and evolving. It is looking for collaboration with people concerned, associations, institutions, tourism actors and public authorities.
